# Software Development Methodology and System Models

**OWWA Region IV-A Inventory Management System**
Capstone / Thesis — Academic Write-up

---

## System Overview

The OWWA Region IV-A Inventory Management System is a web-based application developed to support the Overseas Workers Welfare Administration (OWWA) Regional Office IV-A in the systematic management of office supplies and assets. The system is designed to replace the agency's manual, paper-based or spreadsheet-driven inventory and requisition processes with a centralized, auditable, and role-governed digital workflow. It supports three distinct user roles — Supply Custodian, Unit Head, and Employee — each with defined responsibilities, navigation access, and data visibility. The Supply Custodian manages the entire inventory lifecycle, including the recording of acquisitions, issuances, item transfers between offices, and disposals, as well as the approval or rejection of consolidated requisitions. The Unit Head serves as the intermediate officer who reviews and compiles the individual requisitions submitted by employees within their office into a single, unified request before forwarding it to the Supply Custodian. The Employee, on the other hand, is limited to submitting supply requests and tracking the status of those requests within the system.

Beyond transactional operations, the system provides analytical capabilities such as consumption trend charting per department or office, low-stock alerts based on user-defined reorder levels, and exportable reports aligned with Commission on Audit (COA) reporting requirements. An optional artificial intelligence component — implemented through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) combined with a large language model — assists the Supply Custodian by analyzing historical consumption and stock data to generate procurement recommendations, including priority levels, suggested reorder quantities, and reasons for each recommendation. Together, these features aim to improve the efficiency, transparency, and accountability of inventory management operations at OWWA Region IV-A.

## Software Development Process

The development of the system followed a structured, phased software development lifecycle with iterative refinements throughout its execution. The process began with requirements analysis, during which stakeholder needs, role definitions, and functional requirements were gathered and documented. This phase involved identifying the key actors in the inventory management process — namely the Supply Custodian, the Unit Head, and the Employee — and defining the data flows and operational steps associated with each role. The outputs of this phase served as the foundation for the design activities that followed.

During the design phase, the system's processes, data flows, and data structures were modeled using several standard diagramming tools and techniques. Data Flow Diagrams (DFD) at Level 0 and Level 1 were prepared to define the system boundary and to illustrate how data moves between external entities and internal processes. Process flowcharts were developed for each user role to document the step-by-step operational workflows of the proposed system. An Entity-Relationship Diagram (ERD) was constructed to represent the logical database structure, including all entities, their attributes, and the relationships among them. Use case modeling was also performed to enumerate the system functions available to each actor. The architectural design of the system follows the Model-View-Controller (MVC) pattern inherent in the Laravel framework, supplemented by a service layer responsible for business logic such as stock computation, consumption analytics, and AI recommendation generation.

The implementation phase translated the design specifications into a functional web application built on the Laravel 12 framework, using Filament 5 as the server-driven UI framework for building the administrative panel and resource management interfaces. Supporting services were developed to handle inventory stock computation, consumption analytics, and AI-assisted procurement recommendations. The testing and evaluation phase involved functional testing of system features, validation of role-based access controls, and a quality evaluation grounded in the ISO/IEC 25010 software quality model. Finally, the deployment and documentation phase ensured that the system was made accessible to end users and that technical and user-facing documentation was prepared to support operation and maintenance.

## Process Flowcharts

Process flowcharts were developed to describe the operational workflow followed by each of the three user roles when interacting with the proposed system. Flowcharts are widely used in systems analysis and design to illustrate the sequence of activities, decision points, and terminal states in a given process, making them particularly useful for comparing the current, manual process with the proposed system.

The Supply Custodian flowchart begins with the user logging into the system and proceeding to the dashboard, where low-stock alerts are immediately visible. The custodian may then choose from several operational tasks: recording a new acquisition by entering the item, quantity, source, and cost; handling a pending requisition by reviewing its details and deciding to approve or reject it, with approved requisitions proceeding to item release and issuance; recording an inter-office transfer; logging a disposal; running an AI procurement analysis by selecting a category and review period; or generating and downloading a COA report. Each path ends in a clearly defined terminal state, ensuring that all possible outcomes are accounted for.

The Unit Head flowchart reflects the intermediate role of this user in the requisition process. Upon logging in, the Unit Head may check stock levels for their assigned office or review the list of pending requisitions submitted by employees within their office. After reviewing individual employee requests, the Unit Head compiles all accepted items into a single consolidated requisition, reviews and finalizes the list, and submits it to the Supply Custodian. The Unit Head then monitors the status of the submitted requisition; if it is fulfilled, the items are received and distributed to employees, and if it is rejected, the remarks are read and the request may be revised and resubmitted.

The Employee flowchart depicts the most streamlined role in the system. The employee may either submit a new requisition by filling in the required items and quantities or track the status of an existing request. After submission, the employee waits for a decision from the Unit Head. If the request is approved and eventually fulfilled, the employee collects the issued items; if rejected, the employee reads the provided remarks and may choose to resubmit. These three role-specific flowcharts collectively document the proposed business process and provide a basis for comparing it against the current manual workflow.

## Data Flow Diagrams (DFD Level 0 and Level 1)

The Data Flow Diagram (Level 0) illustrates the OWWA Region IV-A Inventory Management System architecture and its interactions with three primary user groups: the Supply Custodian, the Unit Head, and the Employee. The system operates as an integrated platform that provides stock management, requisition processing, stock monitoring, AI-assisted procurement analysis, and report generation capabilities.

The Supply Custodian interacts with the system through three main data flows that each have a clearly paired system response. First, the custodian sends transaction details covering acquisitions, issuances, transfers, and disposals; in return, the system computes and returns stock alerts and stock level summaries based on those transactions. Second, the custodian sends an AI analysis request, and the system responds with AI procurement recommendations derived from historical inventory data. Third, the custodian submits a report request, and the system generates and returns COA-oriented reports. Together, these paired flows enable the Supply Custodian to maintain full oversight and control over the agency's inventory operations.

The Unit Head interacts with the system by submitting a consolidated requisition — a compiled, unified supply request derived from the individual requests of employees within the Unit Head's office. The system responds by providing the Unit Head with the current status of submitted requisitions and a view of stock levels scoped to their assigned office, allowing them to monitor procurement outcomes and current supply availability without exposure to system-wide data.

The Employee interacts with the system by submitting individual requisition requests specifying the items and quantities needed. The system returns the current status of those submitted requests, informing the employee whether their request is pending, approved, fulfilled, or rejected, and enabling them to act accordingly.

The Level 0 diagram establishes the system boundary by defining precisely what data enters and exits the system and who is responsible for initiating or receiving each data flow. It also makes clear the hierarchical nature of the requisition process: the Employee submits a request, the Unit Head consolidates and forwards it, and the Supply Custodian acts upon the consolidated submission.

The Level 1 Data Flow Diagram provides a breakdown of the OWWA Inventory Management System's internal structure and the data interactions among its major processes and data stores. This level decomposes the single process of Level 0 into six functional sub-processes and introduces five data stores that record and provide the information needed to support system operations.

The system structure at Level 1 is organized around six processes. Process 1.0, Authenticate User, handles the verification of login credentials against the Users data store (D1) for all three actors before any other operation can proceed. Process 2.0, Manage Stock, is the primary operational process used by the Supply Custodian to record all inventory transactions — acquisitions, issuances, transfers, and disposals — writing these records into the Inventory Transactions data store (D3). Upon the approval of a requisition through Process 3.0, the approved data is forwarded to Process 2.0 to trigger the corresponding issuance. Process 3.0, Process Requisitions, receives consolidated requisitions from the Unit Head and individual requests from the Employee, reads item reference data from the Items data store (D2), and writes requisition records to the Requisitions data store (D4). It returns requisition status updates to both the Unit Head and the Employee. Process 4.0, Monitor Stock, reads from the Inventory Transactions data store (D3) and the Items data store (D2) to compute current stock levels and compare them against reorder levels; it issues stock alerts and stock-level views to the Supply Custodian and the Unit Head respectively. Process 5.0, AI Analysis, reads historical inventory transaction data from D3 and generates AI procurement run records in the AI Runs data store (D5), returning procurement recommendations — including suggested quantities, priorities, and justifications — to the Supply Custodian. Process 6.0, Generate Reports, reads from both the Inventory Transactions data store (D3) and the Requisitions data store (D4) to produce COA-aligned reports delivered to the Supply Custodian.

The system organizes and manages information across five data stores. D1 (Users) maintains user credentials, roles, and office or department assignments, and is referenced by the authentication process. D2 (Items) stores the item master data, including item names, categories, units of measurement, and reorder levels, and is referenced by both the requisition and stock monitoring processes. D3 (Inventory Transactions) captures all recorded stock movements — acquisitions, issuances, transfers, and disposals — and serves as the primary data source for stock computation, analytics, and report generation. D4 (Requisitions) holds all requisition records and their line items, status updates, and approval information, supporting both the requisition process and report generation. D5 (AI Runs) stores the results of each AI procurement analysis, including the suggested items, offices, quantities, and priorities, enabling the Supply Custodian to review and reference past recommendations.

In summary, the Level 1 DFD demonstrates how the OWWA Inventory Management System achieves structured data management and role-governed operations through its modular process design and organized data stores. The clear separation of stock management, requisition handling, monitoring, AI analysis, and reporting functions ensures that each user role interacts only with the processes and data relevant to their responsibilities, reducing the risk of unauthorized access and maintaining the integrity of the agency's inventory records.

## Entity-Relationship Diagram (ERD)

The Entity-Relationship Diagram (ERD) of the OWWA Region IV-A Inventory Management System illustrates how the system's data is structured and how each part is connected. The central table is the users table, which stores the name, email, password, and role of every system user — Supply Custodian, Unit Head, or Employee — and links each user to an office and an optional department. The offices and departments tables provide the organizational structure that all other tables reference, ensuring every transaction and request is tied to its proper location within the agency.

Inventory items are organized through two related tables. The item_categories table classifies items into groups such as consumables, semi-expendable, and power plant equipment, while the items table records each item's name, unit, item code, reorder level, and category. The reorder level is particularly significant as it serves as the threshold for low-stock detection throughout the system.

The requisition process spans two tables. The requisitions table records each supply request with its office, department, requesting user, status, and approving user. Each requisition links to one or more rows in the requisition_items table, where each row specifies an item and the quantity requested, allowing a single consolidated requisition from a Unit Head to cover multiple items. Inventory movement is then tracked across four transaction tables — acquisitions for stock-in, issuances for item releases, transfers for inter-office movements, and disposals for write-offs — each linked to the relevant item, office, and recording user. Together, these four tables form the complete transaction history from which the system computes current stock levels.

Finally, the AI procurement feature is supported by the ai_procurement_runs table, which records each analysis execution, and the ai_procurement_items table, which stores the suggested items, offices, quantities, and priorities generated by the AI. Supporting tables include fiscal_years for defining report periods and rag_embeddings for storing the vector data used by the AI recommendation component. Overall, the ERD serves as the structural foundation that connects users, organizational units, inventory items, supply requests, and transaction records in a consistent and auditable design.

## Use Case Description

The use case model identifies the actors of the system and enumerates the functions available to each actor. It provides a user-centered perspective that complements the data-centered DFDs and the sequence-centered flowcharts.

The system has three actors. The Supply Custodian is the primary actor responsible for managing the entire inventory lifecycle. This actor can log into the system, view the dashboard with system-wide statistics, record acquisitions, transfers, and disposals, approve or reject consolidated requisitions submitted by Unit Heads, issue items to offices and departments, run AI procurement analysis and review recommendations, and generate COA-oriented reports. The Unit Head is an intermediate actor whose primary responsibility is the consolidation of employee supply requests. This actor can log in, view stock levels scoped to their assigned office, review pending employee requisitions, compile selected requests into a single consolidated requisition and submit it to the Supply Custodian, track the status of submitted requisitions, issue items to employees within their office, and create or manage Employee accounts within their office. The Employee is the end-user actor who initiates the supply request process. This actor can log in, view their assigned office's stock levels and consumption data, submit a requisition specifying the items and quantities needed, and track the status of their submitted requests.

This use case model defines the scope of the system by making explicit what functions each actor can and cannot perform, and supports the derivation of test cases to verify that role-based access controls are correctly implemented.

## Algorithm Description and Practical Application

The system employs several algorithms to support its stock monitoring, consumption analytics, and AI-assisted procurement recommendation features. These algorithms are implemented in dedicated service classes and are applied throughout the system to compute derived values from raw transaction data.

The stock computation algorithm calculates the current stock level for each item-and-office combination by aggregating all relevant inventory transactions. Specifically, current stock is determined as the sum of all acquisition quantities plus the sum of all incoming transfer quantities, minus the sum of all issuance quantities, the sum of all outgoing transfer quantities, and the sum of all disposal quantities. This formula ensures that stock levels accurately reflect all movements recorded in the system, from initial procurement to final disposal. The low-stock detection algorithm then compares each computed stock level against the item's configured reorder level; an item-and-office pair is flagged as low stock when the current stock is at or below the reorder level and the reorder level is greater than zero. These flags are surfaced on the dashboard as alerts and counts, scoped to the user's role and office assignment.

For consumption analytics, the system uses a Simple Moving Average (SMA) algorithm to smooth the consumption trend data displayed in charts. Given a time-ordered series of consumption values and a window size N, the SMA at each position is computed as the arithmetic mean of the N preceding values. This smoothing reduces the visual noise caused by irregular or seasonal consumption patterns, making trends easier to interpret. The consumption rate algorithm computes the average quantity consumed per time period by dividing the total quantity issued over a given range by the number of periods in that range, yielding a value expressed in units per month. This rate forms the basis for estimating the "months of cover" figure, which indicates how many months the current stock will last at the current consumption rate. A linear trend slope algorithm, based on the method of least squares, is applied to a time-ordered series of consumption values to determine whether consumption is increasing or decreasing over time; a positive slope indicates growing demand while a negative slope indicates a decline. A period-over-period growth calculation is also provided, expressing the percentage change from the earliest to the most recent period in a consumption series, offering a summary measure of long-term change in usage.

These algorithms collectively support three of the six DFD Level 1 processes: Monitor Stock (P4), which uses stock computation and low-stock detection; Generate Reports (P6), which incorporates consumption rates and stock levels into COA report outputs; and AI Analysis (P5), which uses computed months-of-cover values and reorder level comparisons as context for the large language model when generating procurement recommendations. The combination of deterministic algorithms for stock and analytics with a language model for recommendation narrative generation represents the core technical contribution of the proposed system.
